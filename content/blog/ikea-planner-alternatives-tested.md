# IKEA Room Planner Alternatives Tested in 2026 (By Someone Who Actually Used Them)

Let me tell you exactly how this comparison was run.

I took a real room — a 14×11 living room with one bay window, a radiator on the south wall, and a doorway offset from center — and planned it in six different tools. Same room. Same furniture wish list (a mix of IKEA pieces and one Article sofa). Same goal: a workable layout I could actually use.

The IKEA planner handled the IKEA furniture fine. It had no idea what to do with the Article sofa. The bay window required workarounds. The radiator placement was possible but fiddly.

This is not an unusual room. Most rooms have at least one non-rectangular feature. Most people buy from more than one brand. The IKEA planner is free and it's convenient — but it's optimized for a shopping session, not for designing a real space you live in.

Here's what the alternatives actually produced.

## Why People Look for IKEA Planner Alternatives (The Real Reasons)

Before the tools: the specific frustrations that drive people to search for alternatives. Which one applies to you will determine which tool is the right answer.

**"I'm mixing IKEA with other brands."**
The IKEA planner's furniture library is entirely IKEA. If you own a sofa from somewhere else, it doesn't exist in the tool. You can use a generic rectangle as an approximation, but then you're not actually seeing your room — you're seeing a placeholder room.

**"My room isn't a rectangle."**
Bay windows, alcoves, chimney breasts, L-shaped spaces, angled walls. The IKEA planner can technically handle some of these, but non-standard geometry is slow and imprecise. You spend more time fighting the room shape than planning the layout.

**"The tool is slow."**
The 3D view in the IKEA planner loads slowly and can become frustrating to navigate on anything but a very fast connection. For a tool you might spend an hour with, this friction compounds.

**"There's no help — I don't know if my layout is actually good."**
The IKEA planner tells you if furniture physically overlaps. It has no opinion about whether your layout makes sense spatially, whether the traffic flow works, or whether the proportions are correct. You're completely on your own.

**"I need to share this with someone."**
The sharing options in the IKEA planner are limited to a link that requires the recipient to open the same session. No clean PDF export, no shareable image with furniture labels, nothing you'd hand to a partner or contractor.

## The Tools: What They Actually Delivered

### IKEA Room Planner
**What it is:** browser-based floor plan tool tied to IKEA's full product catalog
**Cost:** free

In the test room: the IKEA pieces went in quickly. The search function is good and the furniture catalog is deep. The 3D view looks impressive — IKEA's product renders are high quality. The bay window required drawing it as a small rectangular bump, which worked but slightly misrepresented the actual shape.

The Article sofa: doesn't exist. I placed a generic 90-inch rectangle and continued. This is the core limitation — the plan no longer shows my room, it shows IKEA's interpretation of my room with a rectangle where my actual sofa goes.

**Best use case:** planning a room you're furnishing entirely or mostly from IKEA. For that use case specifically, nothing beats it.

---

### Muebly
**What it is:** AI-assisted room planner with multi-brand furniture catalog
**Cost:** free to start

In the test room: room entry was fast — input the dimensions, place doors, windows, and the radiator as a fixed object. The bay window was handled correctly with the polygon room tool. The Article sofa was searchable and placed at its real dimensions. The IKEA pieces were also in the catalog.

The AI layout suggestion: after placing the major pieces, I ran the AI layout assistant. It flagged that the sofa was blocking partial light from the bay window and suggested a 12-degree rotation that improved both the light and the conversational angle with the chairs. This is the kind of spatial feedback the IKEA planner can't provide.

The 3D render: photorealistic and fast. The render correctly showed the radiator on the south wall and how the sofa related to it — something no other tool in this test handled without manual workarounds.

**Best use case:** mixed-brand rooms, any room with non-standard geometry, anyone who wants spatial feedback rather than just a digital canvas.

---

### RoomSketcher
**What it is:** floor-plan-focused tool with 3D visualization
**Cost:** free tier available; professional exports require paid plan (~$49/year)

In the test room: the best floor plan tool in the test. Drawing the room — including the bay window — was intuitive and precise. Dimension snapping worked cleanly. The result is a proper architectural floor plan that a contractor would recognize and trust.

Where it fell short: the furniture library is generic. The Article sofa doesn't exist. The IKEA pieces aren't in it either — everything is a styled generic shape. For planning purposes this is workable. For understanding how your actual furniture will look, it's limited.

The export was the standout: clean PDF with labeled furniture and dimensions. Excellent for sharing with a contractor, partner, or anyone who needs to understand the plan without opening the tool.

**Best use case:** renovation planning, anything that involves sharing with tradespeople, situations where floor plan precision matters more than photorealistic renders.

---

### Planner 5D
**What it is:** full-featured 3D design tool with high-quality renders
**Cost:** limited free tier; paid plans from ~$9.99/month

In the test room: the most powerful tool in the test for pure visualization. The 3D renders are the best available in this category — photorealistic quality that approaches professional architectural visualization. The room shape tools handle irregular geometry well.

The limitations: the learning curve is real. It took me approximately 40 minutes before I felt productive in the tool. The furniture library is large but generic — no specific brand products, just styled generic shapes. The free tier is limited enough that most useful features require a subscription.

**Best use case:** people who want to produce impressive renders — for presentations, for showing a vision to a designer, for making a case to a partner. Not the right tool for quick planning sessions or for working with specific real-world furniture.

---

### Homestyler
**What it is:** AI-first design tool with photo-to-render capabilities
**Cost:** free with optional premium tier

In the test room: the fastest entry point of any tool in the test. The AI room-mapping feature photographed the room and automatically generated approximate dimensions — impressive in concept, less precise in practice (my dimensions were about 8% off before manual correction).

The style generation is genuinely good — you can see the room in different aesthetics quickly and the results are visually compelling. But this is an inspiration tool, not a planning tool. The AI doesn't help you think about layout logic, traffic flow, or furniture scale. It shows you a styled version of a room; it doesn't help you plan one.

**Best use case:** early inspiration phase, when you don't yet know what style direction you want. Not useful once you're in the precision planning phase.

---

### SketchUp Free
**What it is:** professional 3D modeling software with a free web version
**Cost:** free web version; full features require ~$299/year

In the test room: unlimited capability, steep price to access it. The free web version handles complex geometry including the bay window precisely. SketchUp can model anything — if you can draw it, you can model it.

The practical barrier: it took approximately 2 hours of learning before I produced something useful in this tool. The furniture library requires either creating models from scratch or downloading from third-party repositories. For a homeowner who wants to plan a room in an afternoon, SketchUp is the wrong tool.

**Best use case:** technically inclined users, anyone who needs to model unusual architecture, people who enjoy the modeling process and are willing to invest time in learning the tool.

## What the IKEA Planner Does That Alternatives Don't

This is worth saying clearly: the IKEA planner has genuine strengths that alternatives don't fully replicate.

**Catalog depth:** IKEA's full product catalog, updated in real time, with accurate dimensions, colors, and price information for every SKU. No alternative has this for IKEA products specifically.

**Seamless buying flow:** from planning to cart in one session. The IKEA planner's integration with the IKEA shopping experience is native and frictionless. No alternative tool puts you this close to purchase.

**Simplicity:** the IKEA planner is easy to start. No account required for basic use, no learning curve, immediately productive. For a 30-minute session where you want to see if that PAX configuration fits, it's the fastest tool in this list.

The alternatives are better at everything else. But "everything else" might not be your use case.
