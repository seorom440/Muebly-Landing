# RoomGPT vs Muebly: What's the Actual Difference (And Which One to Use)

RoomGPT had a moment. In 2023 it went viral because it did something nobody had seen before: you uploaded a photo of your room, and it gave you back a photorealistic version of that room in a completely different style. Millions of people tried it. Millions were impressed.

And then millions ran into the same wall: the render was beautiful. But you couldn't do anything with it.

You couldn't identify the furniture in the image. You couldn't buy it. You couldn't test a different layout — just a different aesthetic. You couldn't know whether the sofa in the render would actually fit in your room. The render was an endpoint, not a starting point.

This is the gap that defines the RoomGPT alternative search. People aren't looking for a better RoomGPT — they're looking for the next step that RoomGPT doesn't provide.

This guide breaks down what that next step is, which tools actually provide it, and how to choose based on what you're actually trying to accomplish.

---

## What RoomGPT Does Well

Before the alternatives: RoomGPT earned its popularity genuinely. Understanding why helps clarify what you should look for next.

**Speed.** Upload a photo, select a style, get a render in seconds. The feedback loop is immediate and satisfying. No learning curve, no account required for basic use, no setup.

**Visual quality at the style level.** RoomGPT's renders are good at capturing an aesthetic direction. If you want to see your room in Scandinavian, maximalist, or mid-century modern, the output communicates that style convincingly.

**Viral shareability.** The before-and-after comparison format is naturally shareable — it's the same format that made the tool go viral in the first place. For inspiration and social sharing, it works.

**Zero friction entry.** No account, no payment, immediate output. This lowers the barrier to start, which matters for a tool people approach with low commitment.

These are real strengths. The problem is that they're all in service of one thing: visualizing a style. Not planning a room. Not buying furniture. Not making decisions.

---

## Where RoomGPT Falls Short

### The Shopping Layer Is Missing

RoomGPT generates AI art. The furniture in the output is not real furniture. You cannot identify what sofa is in the render, find it at a retailer, check if it comes in a color that works for your walls, or know whether it fits your room.

This is the most cited frustration from RoomGPT users — and it's structural, not a feature gap that can be patched. The tool was built around style generation, not product selection.

### There's No Spatial Planning

A RoomGPT render is aesthetically transformed but spatially identical to your original photo. The layout stays the same. If your living room has the sofa in the wrong position, the render shows you a different-looking sofa in the wrong position.

Real room design requires changing the layout, not just the visual treatment. RoomGPT can't help with where things go.

### You Can't Work With Real Dimensions

There's no way to tell RoomGPT your room is 14×11, that the window is on the north wall, or that the radiator limits where the sofa can go. The tool doesn't know your room — it knows your photo. These are different things.

### The Output Is One-Use

You get a render. What do you do with it? You can look at it, share it, and compare it to your current room. Then what? There's no path from "I like this look" to "here's what to buy and where to put it."
