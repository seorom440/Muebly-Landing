# How to Decorate a Rental Apartment: What You Can Actually Change (Without Losing Your Deposit)

Most rental decorating advice falls into one of two categories: things you're technically not supposed to do but can probably get away with, or things so cautious they barely change anything. Neither is particularly useful.

This guide takes a different approach: what can you genuinely and legally do in a standard rental, what impact does each change have, and what does it cost to undo when you move out.

The framing matters because rental decorating is a reversibility problem. Every decision needs a clean exit. The techniques that work best are the ones with high visual impact and a straightforward removal process — not hacks that leave you anxious every time you look at them.

---

## What the Lease Actually Restricts (And What It Doesn't)

Most standard residential leases prohibit: structural modifications, electrical modifications, and alterations that can't be reversed to the original state. Almost everything else is available with varying degrees of landlord approval.

**What standard leases prohibit:**
- Painting walls (most leases; some allow it if you repaint to original color on departure)
- Installing hardwired lighting or ceiling fans
- Removing or altering built-in fixtures (cabinets, shelving, doors)
- Drilling into structural elements
- Wallpaper with adhesive backing that damages drywall on removal

**What standard leases allow without restriction:**
- Area rugs over existing flooring
- Furniture of any configuration
- Removable wall solutions (Command strips, removable wallpaper, peel-and-stick tiles)
- Window treatments that use tension rods or existing hardware
- Floor lamps, plug-in pendants, plug-in wall sconces
- All soft furnishings: curtains, pillows, throws, bedding

**Worth asking your landlord about:**
- Painting (many landlords allow it; requires repainting to original on departure)
- Removable wallpaper (most modern removable products leave no residue; many landlords permit)
- Picture hanging with small nails (standard small nail holes are considered normal wear in most jurisdictions)

The ask-first list is worth pursuing because the answer is more often yes than most renters assume — particularly for changes you'll reverse before leaving.

## The Changes With the Highest Impact in Rentals

### 1. Lighting Overhaul ($50–$200)

The single highest-impact change available in a rental, and fully within the scope of what leases permit: replace the lighting.

Standard rental apartments come with basic overhead fixtures and minimal thought given to light quality. The fix doesn't require any electrical work:

**Plug-in pendants:** standard pendant lights that plug into a wall outlet and drape a cord to hang above a dining table or beside a bed. They look like hardwired pendant lights. They're not. They remove in seconds.

**Plug-in wall sconces:** same principle — wall-mounted look, plug-in installation. Available in styles from $25 to $250. They require a small Command strip or a single small nail for the backing plate.

**Bulb temperature:** replace all bulbs in existing overhead fixtures with 2700K warm white versions. This changes the color of every surface in the apartment and typically takes 15 minutes. The visual effect is significant.

**Floor and table lamps:** introduce at least one per main room. Turn off the overhead in the evenings and use only lamps. This is the change most renters underestimate — the difference in atmosphere is larger than almost any furniture change.

---

### 2. Curtain Upgrade ($60–$300 per room)

Standard rental window treatments are functional at best. A curtain upgrade transforms the proportions of a room — and with the right approach, leaves no trace on departure.

**The tension rod solution:** in most windows, a tension rod fits between the window frame and holds curtains without a single hole. This works for windows up to approximately 48 inches wide with medium-weight curtains.

**For wider windows or heavier curtains:** a curtain rod with Command-strip compatible brackets, or small nails with wall anchors that fill cleanly with spackle on departure.

**The ceiling-height rule:** hang curtains as high as possible — as close to the ceiling as the window allows. This visually extends the window height and makes the ceiling appear higher. This is one of the most visually powerful changes available in a rental, and it costs no more than hanging curtains at window height.

**Blackout lining:** if the rental curtains or blinds are sheer or minimal, adding blackout-lined curtains transforms the bedroom's usability. Clip-on blackout liners are available that attach to existing curtain rings — no sewing, no modification.

---

### 3. Area Rugs as Flooring Replacement ($80–$400)

Most rental apartments have serviceable but aesthetically neutral floors: beige carpet, laminate, basic tile. A large area rug over the existing floor is the most legally unambiguous design change available — it covers what you don't like and defines the space as designed.

**The sizing rule:** as discussed in other guides, renters consistently choose rugs too small. In a rental living room, an 8×10 or 9×12 rug that covers the seating group is dramatically more effective than a smaller rug that only covers the coffee table area.

**Rug layering:** placing a smaller, patterned rug over a larger, neutral base rug creates depth and personality while keeping the overall treatment removable. This is particularly effective in rental bedrooms where the floor might be uninspiring carpet.

**Rug pads:** non-slip rug pads extend the life of the rug and prevent any discoloration from the rug's backing on light-colored floors. Use them always — they protect the rental floor as much as they protect the rug.

### 4. Removable Wallpaper and Wall Solutions ($40–$150 per wall)

Removable wallpaper has improved significantly in recent years. Modern versions use a dry adhesive that peels cleanly from most painted drywall surfaces — leaving no residue and no surface damage when removed correctly.

**Where it works best:**
- An accent wall in a bedroom (typically the wall behind the headboard)
- A kitchen backsplash area with peel-and-stick tile panels
- An entryway or hallway wall where a pattern creates strong visual impact

**Where to be careful:**
- Freshly painted walls — the paint needs to have cured fully (at least 30 days) before removable wallpaper is applied; fresh paint can peel with the wallpaper
- Textured walls — removable wallpaper doesn't adhere well to strong texture and removal can be more difficult
- Check adhesion in a small corner first and test removal before covering the full wall

The visual impact of a single patterned or textured wall is significant enough that it's usually the first thing people notice in a rental that looks designed versus one that looks like it's waiting for the tenants to personalize it.

### 5. Furniture Investment Strategy

Rental decorating is where furniture investment strategy matters most: you're buying furniture that you'll move multiple times, possibly to spaces with completely different dimensions. The wrong furniture is not just aesthetically bad — it's physically incompatible with future apartments.

**Invest in neutrally-sized furniture:**
- A sofa at 84–90 inches fits most living rooms; a 110-inch sectional doesn't
- Round dining tables work in more configurations than rectangular ones of equivalent seating
- Drawer units and bookshelves that don't depend on specific ceiling heights

**Don't invest in apartment-specific solutions:**
- Built-in shelving that fits perfectly but only works in this apartment's specific alcove dimensions
- Furniture that perfectly fills an odd-shaped space — it won't fill any other odd-shaped space the same way

## The Departure Plan: How to Undo Everything

Every rental decorating decision should have a documented exit strategy. The most common departure mistakes:

**Paint touch-up:** if you've painted (with permission), purchase the original paint color before moving in and keep it. Touch up scuffs and marks before inspection. Don't assume white is white — original colors vary significantly.

**Small nail holes:** most jurisdictions classify small nail holes (standard picture-hanging nails) as normal wear. Fill them with a small amount of white toothpaste or wall putty that matches the wall color. Sand lightly and paint over if needed.

**Command strip removal:** pull the tab straight down slowly, not outward. This is the correct removal technique that prevents surface damage. If a patch of paint comes with it, apply a small amount of matching paint or spackle.

**Removable wallpaper:** pull from a corner at a very low angle (almost parallel to the wall) slowly. Have a hair dryer available to warm stubborn sections — heat releases the adhesive without damaging the paint.

**Rug marks on carpet:** furniture legs can leave depressions in carpet after extended periods. Steam clean or use an ice cube on carpet depressions — the fibers will generally recover. Rug pads prevent this problem by distributing weight.

## What's Worth Asking Your Landlord to Approve

These changes are often permitted but require explicit landlord approval. The ask is worth making — approval rates are higher than most renters expect:

**Painting:** offer to repaint to the original color on departure. Many landlords will approve this — they'd rather have you paint than deal with dinged walls. Get the approval in writing.

**Ceiling fan or light fixture replacement:** offer to keep the original fixtures in the apartment for reinstallation on departure. Many landlords will approve fixture changes that are style improvements, especially if you keep the originals.

**Shelving with wall anchors:** explain the anchors and offer to fill holes on departure. In most cases, the landlord cares about the walls being restored, not about whether there were holes during the tenancy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I paint a rental apartment?**
It depends on your lease. Standard leases prohibit painting. However, many landlords will approve painting if asked in advance and you agree to repaint to the original color on departure. Get the approval in writing before proceeding.

**How do I hang art without drilling holes?**
Command picture-hanging strips support artwork up to approximately 16 lbs per pair. For heavier pieces, a single small nail with a wall anchor, filled and touched up on departure, is usually within the scope of normal wear. For very heavy artwork, a floor-leaning arrangement (artwork leaned against the wall on a shelf or directly on the floor) avoids the question entirely.

**What flooring options work over rental carpet?**
Area rugs are the primary option — they're removable and require no installation. Interlocking foam or cork tiles are an alternative for a desk area or exercise space, though check that they don't discolor the carpet beneath. Hard flooring overlays are generally not appropriate for rentals.

**Is removable wallpaper really fully removable?**
From properly prepared drywall that has been painted with standard latex paint and allowed to cure, yes — modern removable wallpaper products from reputable brands (Chasing Paper, Tempaper, Peel and Stick Mural) remove cleanly. From freshly painted walls, textured walls, or walls with older paint, removal can pull surface paint. Test a small section first.

**How do I make a rental apartment feel like my own without overspending on furniture I'll have to move?**
Focus on the soft furnishings (rugs, curtains, throw pillows, bedding) that add personality at lower cost and are easy to transport. Invest in lighting, which transforms the atmosphere of any space and travels easily. Reserve major furniture spending for pieces that are neutral in size and style enough to work in multiple future apartments.

**What's the single best deposit-safe investment for a rental?**
A large area rug in the living room. It covers unappealing flooring, defines the seating zone, adds warmth and sound absorption, and removes without a trace. The visual impact relative to cost is higher than any other rental-safe intervention.

## Conclusion

Rental decorating is a reversibility design challenge — every intervention has to have a clean exit. Within that constraint, the available options are more substantial than most renters realize: lighting, curtains, rugs, removable wallpaper, and a furniture strategy that anticipates future moves.

The changes with the highest visual impact and cleanest reversibility, in order: lighting overhaul (lamps, plug-in pendants, bulb temperature), curtains hung at ceiling height, a correctly sized living room rug, and one removable wallpaper accent wall.
